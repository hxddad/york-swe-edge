1st Year Courses

If you are reading this and starting or completing your first year of Software Engineering, welcome aboard! Your first year will contain classic and core engineering courses that are common across all engineering programs. The only difference though is that Software, Computer and Electrical Engineers have to take EECS 1028 , the rest (Mechanical, Civil and Space) will have to take ESSE 1012 in their first year.

ENG 1101 - Ethics, Communication & Problem Solving

This will be your first ever ENG course, which will cover engineering ethics and case studies, communication and problem solving. Straightforward course, but quite time-consuming.

Difficulty: 2/10.



EECS 1011 - Computational Thinking Through Mechatronics (MATLAB)

This will be your very first programming class in your undergraduate studies, with a programming language used by scientists and engineers called MATLAB (short for Matrix Laboratory). This course also assumes you never programmed before. You may be asked to buy an Arduino Uno kit for the final project you'll be assigned at the end of the course. I personally have never used MATLAB after I finished this course. Despite that, if you've never coded before, I would advise you to learn the coding fundamentals (variables, scoping, loops, etc.) for future courses.

Difficulty: 2/10 if known at least one programming language, otherwise 4/10.



MATH 1013 - Applied Calculus 1

Behold your very first calculus course at university. This course touches on differential calculus, which is basically review from high school calculus. Though, you may learn a few new topics and concepts (including a little bit on integrals in MATH 1014). If you've taken AP or IB calculus, the course shouldn't be difficult.

Difficulty: 5/10.



MATH 1025 - Applied Linear Algebra

You will learn about and apply linear algebra concepts such as matrices, vectors, linear transformations, eigenvalues and eigenvectors, etc. It's a useful course if you want to go into Machine Learning. If you've done at least the vectors component from calculus and vectors (MCV4U) from an Ontario high school, you should be fine.

Difficulty: 5/10.



PHYS 1800 - Engineering Mechanics

This first physics course you will be taking will mostly be review high school physics, since it mostly covers mechanics (kinematics, force, energy, etc.). You will also have a laboratory component where you will work on experiments related to course content that are worth your grade. Keep practicing physics problems and treat labs as free marks to get a decent grade.

Difficulty: 6/10.



ENG 1102 - Engineering Design Principles

This ENG course will be your first design course. Just as the course titles says, you'll be taught about engineering designs for solving problems through drawings, frameworks, models, aesthetics, etc. You may also be placed in a group to propose an engineering design as a potential solution for a current world or local problem. Make sure you are focusing on a technical design for a technical problem, rather than a social one. Just like ENG1101, straightforward but time-consuming.

Difficulty: 3/10.



CHEM 1100 - Chemistry & Materials Science

This is your first (and probably last) chemistry course in the program. I mostly found this course to be easier than my grade 12 chemistry class, part of it due to having no organic chemistry. This course requires practice and memorization to do well. You also will do chemistry lab experiments involving titration, polymers, analyzing equilibriums and chemical reactions, ferrofluids, and more. The nice thing about this course is that you can take it anytime, since it's not a prerequisite to any mandatory future courses. If you are looking to do a lighter course load in your second semester of your first year, CHEM 1100 is your pick to opt out for now. Generally, your 2nd semester is harder than the 1st, because of new content that you have never learned in high school, which will be common in 2nd year and beyond. Though, it would be nice to get over it soon as you can, since it may not be offered every semester or you forget most of your chemistry from high school.

Difficulty: 6/10.



EECS 1021 - OOP from Sensors to Actuators (Java)

This is the same premise as EECS 1011, but you will be learning and using the Java programming language along with OOP (Object Oriented Programming) principles. I cannot stress this enough, but take this course religiously. Know the Java syntax and OOP well, it will come in handy for future programming and EECS courses. This course also helped me build my first personal project using the Arduino board and with several Java libraries. Java may be a bit difficult to get used to coming from only knowing MATLAB or a dynamically typed language like Python. I had a Python background, so I had to get used to Java's syntax and features.

Difficulty: 4/10.



MATH 1014 - Applied Calculus 2

This is the hardest calculus (but the most interesting in my opinion) course you will take in your undergrad. You will expand through integral calculus with integration techniques, improper integrals, sequences and series and power series. Practice problems and master course concepts to do well, as you did previously in MATH 1013.

Difficulty: 7/10.



PHYS 1801 - Electricity, Magnetism and Optics

Just as the course title says, you will learning about these things. This is the most crucial physics course (and an interesting one) for future courses such as electrical circuits, digital logic design, etc. You will also be mostly using the breadboard with software such as LabVIEW in the labs. I never used a breadboard prior to this course, so it was a bit tricky for me to use it. Ask your TAs for help if your breadboard isn't working the way it's supposed to after tinkering with it for a while, so you don't burn time during your lab. Comparing the course content with PHYS 1800, I found this to be slightly easier.

Difficulty: 6/10.



EECS 1028 - Discrete Mathematics for Engineers

Let me introduce you to the most infamous course to every first year EECS engineering student. The primary reason this is difficult is because it's different from the computational or plug-n-chug courses you've been doing previously. It is about proofs, which most students struggle with at first. Try to get a decent professor, work with a group of friends, understand the concepts and practice, practice, practice to succeed. Ideally, try to get this course done before you start your 2nd year. I dropped it in my 2nd semester in first year, but then I retook it with a different professor in the summer and got a decent grade, without tanking my EECS GPA. It's also a crucial course that requires you to write proofs and know logic for useful courses like EECS 2101 (Data Structures). Another reason why it's hard because you may be required to learn exclusive concepts taught from course notes and unavailable online to self-learn. I however took the course in the summer where we used a regular published discrete mathematics textbook, instead of foreign course notes.
